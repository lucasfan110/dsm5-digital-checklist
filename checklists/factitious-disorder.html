Factitious Disorder

  1. A. Falsification of physical or psychological signs or symptoms, or induction of injury or disease, associated with identified deception.
  2. B. The individual presents himself or herself to others as ill, impaired, or injured.
  3. C. The deceptive behavior is evident even in the absence of obvious external rewards.
  4. D. The behavior is not better explained by another mental disorder, such as delusional disorder or another psychotic disorder.
  5. Specify:

    Single episode Recurrent episodes (two or more events of falsification of illness and/or induction of injury) Factitious Disorder Imposed on Another F68.A (Previously Factitious Disorder by Proxy)

  6. A. Falsification of physical or psychological signs or symptoms, or induction of injury or disease, in another, associated with identified deception.
  7. B. The individual presents another individual (victim) to others as ill, impaired, or injured.
  8. C. The deceptive behavior is evident even in the absence of obvious external rewards.
  9. D. The behavior is not better explained by another mental disorder, such as delusional disorder or another psychotic disorder.
  10. Note:

    The perpetrator, not the victim, receives this diagnosis.

  11. Specify:

    Single episode Recurrent episodes (two or more events of falsification of illness and/or induction of injury) 368 Recording Procedures When an individual falsifies illness in another (e.g., children, adults, pets), the diagnosis is factitious disorder imposed on another. The perpetrator, not the victim, is given the diagnosis. The victim may be given an abuse diagnosis (e.g., T74.12X; see the chapter “Other Conditions That May Be a Focus of Clinical Attention”). If an individual with factitious disorder imposed on another has also deceptively represented his or her own illness or injury, both factitious disorder imposed on self and on another can be diagnosed.
